How the verification was done, step by step

The work ran like a title examiner's audit crossed with an investigative file. Each step below produced its own verifiable output; every number on this site traces to a saved source capture.

  1. 1

    Where the list came from

    The trail starts on a local blog. In August 2011, the Coatesville Dems blog published a post about the landlord Amrit Lal. Seven years later, on September 16, 2018, an anonymous commenter describing themselves as a former tenant left a comment under that post naming, address by address, the properties they attributed to the family business (archived copy). One of Amrit's grandsons found the comment by googling the family name and typed it into a spreadsheet, mostly to get a sense of scope; that transcription is published here as the-2018-list.csv. The sheet settled how big the portfolio might be. It could not settle whether any line of it was true.

    → 99 addresses claiming ≈$17,017,800 in value and ≈$152,917/mo in rent, all unproven
  2. 2

    Ground rules first

    Before checking a single address we fixed the rules: public records only; a strict source hierarchy (county record beats state registry, which beats estimate sites, which beat the blog); every fact needs a saved artifact; anything unfindable is marked UNVERIFIED instead of guessed. The list itself is never treated as evidence. It is a to-do list.

    → A lead sheet, not a source of truth
  3. 3

    Every address against the county record

    Each of the 99 addresses was matched against Chester County's official parcel layer, the same data the assessment office publishes. That produced a verified parcel ID (UPI), the true owner of record, assessed value, deed date, and last sale price for 88 of them. Eleven addresses simply do not exist in any county database.

    → 88 parcels located · 11 addresses unfindable
  4. 4

    The sweeps that found what the list missed

    Then we inverted the search: instead of checking the list's addresses, we asked the county who else mails their tax bills to the family's PO Box 1944 or 338 Joy Lane, and what else is titled to the family's names and entities. This county-wide sweep surfaced 39 additional parcels the 2018 list never knew about, including two entity names, Maple Ventures and Galway Associates, seen nowhere before.

    → +39 parcels, 2 new entity names
  5. 5

    Taxes, parcel by parcel

    Every family-linked parcel went through the county Tax Claim lookup, 110 individual checks in all. Before trusting a single 'no results' answer, the method was validated against a control parcel with a known delinquent balance, so silence meant 'clean' rather than 'broken search'. Result: zero delinquent parcels. Someone is paying every tax bill.

    → 110/110 current · $0 delinquent
  6. 6

    The entities behind the names

    Every name on county title was checked against the PA Department of State. Three real LLCs exist (ATL Ventures, RST Group, Crestline Investments), but none has ever filed the annual report that would name an owner. The other names on title are registered nowhere at all: ATL Associates, RST Partners, Nagpaul Associates, Maple Ventures, Galway Associates.

    → 3 registered LLCs, 0 disclosed owners · 6+ unregistered names
  7. 7

    The deed chains

    For every property the list called 'sold', we pulled the recorded deed history and traced who actually conveyed it, to whom, when, and for how much. Four were never family-owned at all. One was lost to foreclosure; on another the family turned out to be the lender, not the owner. Nine were sold by the family between 2005 and 2016. Only two family sales happened in the 2020s, and both are fully documented.

    → 17 exits traced, case by case
  8. 8

    The courts

    A sweep of the PA court portal found the rental operation's paper trail: 157 landlord-tenant filings since 2011 with $751,637 in judgments, plus municipal code-citation waves in 2025 and 2026. It proves an active rental business. It also proves that public dockets cannot tell you which specific houses are occupied.

    → 157 filings · $751,637 in judgments · occupancy still unknowable
  9. 9

    Value estimates, clearly labeled

    Redfin and Zillow model estimates were captured for every parcel the models cover: 58 homes worth about $14.7M, and for the 51 of those with a rent model, about $104K a month in market-rate rent potential. These are estimates and are never treated as fact. The other 52 family parcels, mostly vacant land, have no estimate at all, and the data says so out loud.

    → 58 homes valued · 51 with rent estimates · 52 parcels have no public estimate

What it all adds up to

The 2018 list was directionally right about scale and wrong in both directions: 17 of its properties were long gone or never family-owned, and it missed 39 parcels entirely. The verified portfolio is 110 parcels, assessed at $6,031,060. For the 58 homes the pricing models cover, market estimates total ≈$14.7M, about 4.2× those same homes' assessed values. The other 52 parcels, mostly vacant land, have no public market comparison.
THE HEADLINE RISK

22 properties are titled to a man who has died

Amrit Lal died, yet $1,255,810 of assessed real estate is still titled in his name, and the record shows no estate was ever opened. Until probate happens, nobody has clear legal authority to sell, refinance, or properly insure those 22 parcels.

THE STRUCTURAL PROBLEM

Most of the portfolio's paper backbone is missing

Only 27 of 110 parcels sit inside a registered company, and even that LLC has never disclosed an owner. Roughly 45 parcels are titled to names that are registered nowhere. For a lender, a title company, or a buyer, that is a serious conveyance problem waiting at every future closing.

THE GOOD NEWS

Taxes are clean and no assets are leaking

Zero delinquent taxes across all 110 parcels. Every 'sold' property traced to a documented, explainable exit. Zero transfers out of the deceased's name, which also means nothing improper has moved. The records show paperwork neglect, not asset-stripping.

THE OPERATION

A real rental business runs on top of all this

157 eviction and collection filings since 2011, judgments of $751,637, litigated rents of $500 to $3,000 a month, and market-rent potential around $104K a month. The business is real and active. Its books, its rent roll, and its manager's compensation are simply not public.

What public records cannot answer

Four questions matter enormously and appear in no public record anywhere: who actually owns the LLC and in what split; which houses are occupied and at what real rent; each property's cash flow and maintenance cushion; and the manager's effective compensation. Exactly four private document sets answer them: the estate file, the entity tax returns (K-1s), the leases and rent roll, and the bank statements or management agreement. About this site. Every fact here comes from a public government record: county assessment and deed data, the PA Department of State, county Tax Claim lookups, and the PA court portal, each captured as a saved evidence artifact (268 entries). Market values and rents are model estimates, not appraisals. Data as of 2026-07-07; ownership is re-verified against the county weekly. Where a fact could not be verified it is marked Unverified rather than guessed.

Privacy. Court information appears only as aggregates: no tenant is named anywhere on this site, by design. Owners and deed parties are named exactly as they appear in public county records.
